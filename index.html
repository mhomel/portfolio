FOMO, Emotion Regulation & Sleep Quality

Jagiellonian University · 2025 · N = 30 · Pre-post intervention design

Does Fear of Missing Out (FOMO) affect how well young adults sleep — and can emotion regulation strategies buffer that effect? This study examined these questions through a quantitative pre-post design with a 14-day behavioral intervention.

H1
FOMO → Sleep
Confirmed
r = 0.38, p = .039
H2
Moderation
Not confirmed
p > .86
H3
Intervention
Confirmed
p < .05 all

Research Questions

  • Does FOMO correlate with lower sleep quality?
  • Do emotion regulation strategies moderate this relationship?
  • Can a short intervention shift these patterns?

Measures

  • FOMO Scale (Przybylski et al., 2013)
  • Athens Insomnia Scale (AIS)
  • Emotion Regulation Questionnaire (ERQ)
  • Custom habit & demographics surveys

Participants

30 students (18F, 12M), ages 19–26. Jagiellonian University. Inclusion: regular social media use ≥5 days/week.

Intervention

14-day daily exercises: emotion journal, one authentic statement per day, and social comparison reframing.

Statistical Results

FOMO × Sleep quality (Pearson r) r(28) = 0.38, p = .039 ✓
Moderation: cognitive reappraisal B = -0.006, p = .863 ✗
Moderation: expressive suppression B = 0.004, p = .868 ✗
Sleep quality improvement (t-test) t(29) = 2.37, p = .024 ✓
Decrease in emotional suppression t(29) = 6.26, p < .001 ✓
Increase in cognitive reappraisal t(29) = -3.28, p = .003 ✓
Implications for product design The null moderation finding suggests individual coping strategies may not buffer FOMO's impact on sleep — pointing toward a need for product-level interventions such as notification design, social comparison reduction features, and screen time nudges. The intervention's success within 14 days also shows that short, low-effort behavioral prompts can produce measurable psychological change.
Limitations Small convenience sample (N = 30) limits generalizability. FOMO measured post-intervention only. No control group. Self-report bias possible.

Simon's Task

Academic project · 2025 · 3-person collaboration · Python

An experiment based on the Simon effect (Simon & Rudell, 1967), investigating how spatial stimulus-response compatibility affects reaction time and accuracy. Participants respond to the meaning of a word ("LEFT" or "RIGHT") while ignoring its on-screen position — creating congruent and incongruent conditions that reveal how attention interference and automated processing interact.

Design

  • 20-trial training session
  • 4 experimental blocks × 60 randomized trials (240 total)
  • Response times measured with millisecond precision
  • Jittered pause times between trials

Cognitive Focus

  • Spatial stimulus-response compatibility
  • Attention interference
  • Automated processing & executive control

Tools

Python — custom trial logic, randomized generator, full-screen presentation, reaction logging

Team

3-person academic collaboration, Jagiellonian University, Kraków · 2025

UX Design Prototyping Figma Emotional Design

Decision Diary

Personal project · 2025 · Mobile flow · Figma interactive prototype

A calm mobile experience designed to support more reflective decision-making — built for people who tend to overthink or struggle with a sense of control. Rather than pushing users toward quick action, the interface encourages slowing down and identifying what they actually feel.

The Flow

  • Describe the decision
  • Reflect on perceived control
  • Identify the emotion
  • Save to return to later

Design Decisions

  • One question per screen — no overwhelm
  • No time pressure or forced choices
  • Soft visual language, rounded components
  • Subtle progress indicator for sense of control
  • Gentle transitions (Smart Animate, ease-out)

What I Learned

  • How visual hierarchy affects emotional perception
  • How pacing and transitions influence user comfort
  • How to design for reflection rather than speed
  • How small UI decisions change emotional tone

Tools

Figma — UI design, components, auto layout, interactive prototyping

Why this matters for UXR This project sharpened my understanding of how design decisions — spacing, motion, hierarchy — shape how users feel during an interaction. That sensitivity to emotional experience directly informs how I approach research: I'm not just looking at what users do, but how the interface makes them feel while doing it.
