FOMO, Emotion Regulation & Sleep Quality

Jagiellonian University · 2025 · N=30 · Pre-post intervention design

Does Fear of Missing Out affect how well young adults sleep — and can emotion regulation strategies buffer that effect? This study examined these questions through a quantitative pre-post design with a 14-day behavioral intervention, and connected findings to implications for product design.

H1
FOMO → Sleep
Confirmed
r = 0.38, p = .039
H2
Moderation
Not confirmed
p > .86
H3
Intervention
Confirmed
p < .05 all

Research Questions

  • Does FOMO correlate with lower sleep quality?
  • Do emotion regulation strategies moderate this relationship?
  • Can a short intervention shift these patterns?

Measures

  • FOMO Scale (Przybylski et al., 2013)
  • Athens Insomnia Scale (AIS)
  • Emotion Regulation Questionnaire (ERQ)
  • Custom habit & demographics surveys

Participants

30 students (18F, 12M), ages 19–26. Jagiellonian University. Inclusion: regular social media use ≥5 days/week.

Intervention

14-day daily exercises: emotion journal, one authentic statement per day, and social comparison reframing.

Statistical Results

FOMO × Sleep quality (Pearson r) r(28) = 0.38, p = .039 ✓
Moderation: cognitive reappraisal B = -0.006, p = .863 ✗
Moderation: expressive suppression B = 0.004, p = .868 ✗
Sleep quality improvement (t-test) t(29) = 2.37, p = .024 ✓
Decrease in emotional suppression t(29) = 6.26, p < .001 ✓
Increase in cognitive reappraisal t(29) = -3.28, p = .003 ✓
Implications for product design The null moderation finding suggests individual coping strategies may not buffer FOMO's impact on sleep — pointing toward a need for product-level interventions: notification design, social comparison reduction features, screen time nudges. The intervention's success within 14 days shows that short, low-effort behavioral prompts can produce measurable psychological change — a useful framing for product teams designing habit-forming or habit-breaking features.
Limitations Small convenience sample (N=30) limits generalizability. FOMO measured post-intervention only. No control group. Self-report bias possible.

JetBrains Store — Usability Study

Recruitment task · March 2026 · N=1 exploratory session · Remote via Zoom

This was a recruitment task requiring a usability study of the JetBrains Store. The task was intentionally scoped as a single-participant exploratory session — the goal was to demonstrate research process, analytical reasoning, and ability to generate actionable recommendations from limited data, rather than produce generalizable findings.

The participant: a semi-technical user with no prior JetBrains experience. A deliberate choice — non-expert users surface navigation and clarity issues that domain-familiar users tend to overlook automatically.

Core Finding
A semi-technical user could not find a Python development tool — not because it didn't exist, but because the Store speaks in product names, not use cases. He searched for "Python" and found nothing.
High severity

Product-to-use-case mapping is absent. Users who think in terms of tasks ("I want Python") cannot connect to a product (PyCharm) without prior knowledge.

Medium severity

Inconsistent Buy button styling — blue vs white — with no visible functional distinction created confusion about what each button does.

Medium severity

The Students section has no direct CTA. After successfully finding a relevant offer, the user hit a dead end with no way to proceed.

Research Questions

  • How easily can users find the tools they need?
  • Is pricing information clear and discoverable?
  • Are special offers visible and understandable?

Participant

Male, 50s. Semi-technical background, familiar with online purchasing and developer tools broadly, no prior JetBrains experience. Session conducted in Polish to minimise language barriers and support natural think-aloud verbalization.

Tasks

  • Find a tool for learning Python and determine its price
  • Find student discounts or free options for young people
  • Find any promotions or special offers on the Store

Method note

Single-participant exploratory scope per task requirements. Findings serve as hypotheses for future larger-scale testing, not as statistically significant conclusions.

RQ1 — Finding the right tool High severity issue
RQ2 — Pricing clarity Medium severity issue
RQ3 — Special offers & promotions Medium severity issue
"I'm looking for the word Python in these boxes and I can barely see it. I'll probably give up on this page because there are no tools for Python programming here at all." — Participant, Task 1 (translated from Polish)
"Why are some of these buttons blue and some white?" — Participant, Task 2
"The page is readable, but there's no Buy button — at least not on the student section. I don't understand why it's not there." — Participant, end-of-session debrief

Recommendations

  • Add use-case or language labels to product cards (e.g. "For Python development") so users can orient by task, not by product name.
  • Make "For Individual Use" the default landing tab, or add a prominent user-type selector at the top to reduce wrong-audience confusion.
  • Standardise Buy button styling. If two colors serve different functions, add a visible label or tooltip explaining the distinction.
  • Add a clear CTA within the Students section so users know how to proceed after finding a relevant offer.
  • Consider a "What are you trying to do?" entry-point selector that routes users to the right product without requiring JetBrains product knowledge upfront.
Limitations N=1 limits generalizability — findings should be treated as hypotheses to test with a larger sample. Session ran ~11 min (planned 20) as the participant completed tasks efficiently. One moderator prompt was used in Task 2.
